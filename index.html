AI is changing fast. The people it's meant to serve should be the ones shaping it.

This is the story of a collaboration between Centennial College and Community Living Toronto, where people with intellectual and developmental disabilities explored AI alongside researchers and shaped what came out of it.

We discovered how people naturally learn to direct AI, and what becomes possible when AI works to understand people instead of asking people to understand it. People find their voice. Solutions emerge from genuine needs.

This site shares what we did, what we made, and what we discovered.

Nobody was the expert. That turned out to be the point.

We started with interviews — online and in person — getting to know the CLTO influencers and the challenges they face day to day. Then we met. We had casual conversations about what was hard, what would help, and what people were curious about. We explored AI tools together, tried experiments, and paid attention to what happened.

For support workers

How to create the conditions for people to explore AI safely and on their own terms.

The most important thing we learned: don't teach. Create space. Let people bring their real questions. Stay nearby but don't hover. When someone gets stuck, ask what they're trying to do — don't take over the keyboard.

The sessions worked best when they felt like a hangout, not a class. We sat together, tried things, laughed when AI got confused, and celebrated small wins.

For researchers

Our methodology, ethical considerations, and how co-design shaped the research itself.

This project used a participatory action research approach. Community partners were not "subjects" — they were co-researchers. This changed the research questions, the methods, and the outcomes in ways we didn't anticipate.

Ethics approval was obtained through Centennial College. All participants provided informed consent, and consent was revisited throughout the project as activities evolved.

For organizations

What it takes to start a similar collaboration — partnerships, funding, and trust.

The partnership between an academic institution and a community organization was essential. CLTO provided the relationships, the trust, and the understanding of the community. Centennial provided the technical capacity and research framework.

If you're considering something similar, the most important first step is finding a community partner who shares the vision of genuine co-design — not just consultation.

Everyone talks to AI like it's Google. That was the first thing we noticed.

When people first tried AI, they typed short queries — a few words, hit enter, wait for results. But AI isn't a search engine. It's a conversation.

Once we started exploring that together, everything changed. People began talking naturally, sharing context, saying what they actually needed. And a few simple discoveries made a big difference: you can ask it to slow down. You can say "make that simpler." You can interrupt it — you can't hurt its feelings.

Moment

Someone asked AI to explain what it was. The answer came back full of technical jargon — "neural network" this, "language model" that. They asked for simpler terms. AI compared itself to a librarian who's read millions of books. "That makes sense."

Moment

AI was talking too fast, giving overwhelming instructions. Someone said "can you slow down please?" It did, but the content was still too complex. "I'm sorry, I still don't understand what you mean." AI completely changed its approach.

Moment

People naturally waited for AI to finish, even when it was going off track — the way you would with a person. Then someone learned: "You can interrupt it. You can't hurt its feelings." That changed everything.

For support workers

How to help someone shift from "search mode" to natural conversation with AI.

The biggest shift is from typing keywords to talking naturally. You can help by modeling it — talk to AI out loud, show that it's okay to use full sentences, to say "I don't understand," to start over.

Don't correct someone's phrasing. If they say "make it easy," that works just as well as "simplify this." AI adapts. Let people find their own language for directing it.

For researchers

The theoretical framework behind the foundational skills and their emergence patterns.

The foundational skills framework emerged through grounded analysis of session recordings and field notes. We observed consistent patterns across participants, despite varied starting points and goals.

The skills align with but extend existing frameworks in assistive technology adoption, adding the dimension of AI's unique affordances: infinite patience, no judgment, and consequence-free correction.

For app developers

How to embed these skills into your app's interaction patterns.

If you're building AI-powered tools, consider how your interface supports these natural interactions. Can users easily say "make this simpler"? Is there a visible way to interrupt or redirect? Does the app teach these skills through use, not instruction?

The diet tracking app (see Building Together) is our attempt at this. Every design decision was informed by how people naturally direct AI.

Understanding someone first changes everything you build.

Mary had been tracking her diet for years — in eight handwritten notebooks. She knew exactly what she needed: a way to track sodium and fluid intake. But the AI tools she'd tried didn't understand her. They gave generic meal plans. They used complicated interfaces. They didn't listen.

Moment

During a session, we realized: what if we just built it? Right here, right now? The team started prototyping in 20 minutes. Mary watched the screen, then started directing: "No, not like that. Simpler. I just need to drag the food."

What started as a quick experiment became a real tool. Mary went from observer to lead designer. She proposed two separate versions of the app — one for daily tracking, one for the conversation where the app gets to know you. She uses it every day.

Moment

When Mary saw the first working version of her tracker, she said: "This is what I've been doing with eight notebooks." Within minutes she was suggesting changes — not as feedback, but as direction.

For support workers

How to support someone through a co-design process like this one.

The key shift is from building for someone to building with them. Mary didn't just give feedback on what we built — she directed the design. Support workers can help by amplifying the person's voice, not interpreting it.

For researchers

The understanding-first design principle and its implications for AI tool development.

Traditional app design starts with features and works backward to users. Our approach started with understanding — a conversation where the AI learns about the person before presenting any interface. This inverts the typical design process.

For app developers

Technical architecture: RAG integration, conversation design, and accessibility patterns.

The app uses retrieval-augmented generation (RAG) to maintain personal context across sessions. The "getting to know you" conversation produces a structured profile that informs all subsequent interactions. Authentication is simplified to Google SSO to remove the password barrier.

For anyone curious

Why the app talks to you before showing you anything.

Most apps start by showing you a screen full of options. This app starts by asking who you are and what you need. It feels more like meeting someone new than setting up software. That was a deliberate choice — and it came from Mary.

Foundational skills for directing AI

These five skills emerged naturally when people with intellectual and developmental disabilities explored AI on their own terms. They weren't taught — they were discovered.

Comprehension Control

Shaping how AI communicates with you

When AI says something confusing, you don't have to just accept it. You can ask it to try again in simpler words. You're in charge of how the conversation goes.

"Make that simpler" "Say that again" "Use easier words" "I don't understand"

Why it matters: AI defaults to complex language. But it can always simplify — you just have to ask. This one skill unlocks everything else.

Direction Setting

Steering the conversation where you need it to go

AI doesn't always know what you're looking for. You can point it in the right direction by telling it what you want more of, or telling it when it's off track.

"Tell me more about X" "That's not what I'm looking for" "Focus on this part" "Can you give me an example?"

Why it matters: Without direction, AI gives generic answers. With it, AI becomes genuinely useful for your specific situation.

Intention Clarification

Correcting misunderstandings without starting over

Sometimes AI misunderstands what you meant. That's okay. You can correct it without starting the whole conversation over. AI doesn't get frustrated or confused when you redirect.

"That's not what I meant" "Let me try again" "I meant something different" "No, I was asking about..."

Why it matters: With people, constant correction can be awkward. With AI, it's just part of the conversation. You can't hurt its feelings.

Boundary Setting

Deciding what you do and don't want to talk about

You're always in control of the conversation. If AI brings up something you don't want to discuss, you can stop it. If it's going somewhere you don't like, say so.

"Stop" "I don't want to talk about that" "Let's change the subject" "That's too personal"

Why it matters: AI will respect any boundary you set, immediately and without question. This is one of its most important qualities.

Process Control

Managing the pace and structure of the conversation

AI sometimes moves too fast or gives too much at once. You can slow it down, ask it to break things into steps, or tell it to wait while you think.

"Go slower" "Can we break this down?" "One step at a time" "Wait, let me think"

Why it matters: AI has no internal clock. It will wait as long as you need and break anything into smaller pieces. The pace is always yours to set.

Why AI is different

These skills work differently with AI than with people, because AI has qualities that make practice safe and consequence-free:

Infinite patience. AI never gets tired of explaining, never rushes you, and never gets annoyed when you ask the same thing again.

No judgment. AI doesn't think less of you for not knowing something. There's no embarrassment in asking it to simplify.

Consequence-free correction. You can interrupt, redirect, and start over as many times as you need. AI doesn't hold it against you.

Key Insight

These skills emerge best through natural conversation, not instruction. People don't need to be taught these skills — they need a safe space to discover them. The best way to learn to direct AI is to start talking to it about something you actually care about.

Building on these skills

Foundation: The five skills above. Most people discover these naturally within their first few conversations with AI.

Intermediate: Combining skills — like setting direction and controlling pace at the same time. Using AI for longer, more complex tasks. Building confidence to try new things.

Advanced: Using AI as a creative partner. Co-designing tools and workflows. Teaching others what you've learned. Advocating for AI that works the way you need it to.
